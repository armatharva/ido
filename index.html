Step-by-Step Instructions

Follow these simple steps for proper pad application

1

Wash your hands thoroughly with soap and water

2

Remove the pad from its wrapper and unfold it completely

3

Peel off the backing strip to expose the adhesive

4

Center the pad in your underwear with the adhesive side down

5

Press down firmly to ensure the pad sticks properly

6

If the pad has wings, wrap them around the underwear

Remember:

It's completely normal to feel nervous the first few times. With practice, using a pad becomes quick and easy. Don't hesitate to ask a trusted adult for help if you need it!

Choosing the Right Product for You

For Beginners:

Start with disposable pads - they're easiest to learn with and widely available.

For Active Lifestyles:

Tampons or menstrual cups offer the most freedom for sports and swimming.

For Eco-Conscious:

Menstrual cups and period underwear are the most environmentally friendly options.

Menstrual Hygiene Best Practices

Essential hygiene tips to keep you healthy and comfortable during your period

Daily Care

  • Change your pad or tampon every 4-6 hours
  • Wash your hands before and after changing products
  • Use warm water and mild soap for cleaning
  • Always wipe from front to back
  • Shower or bathe daily during your period

Proper Disposal

  • Wrap used pads in toilet paper or their wrapper
  • Never flush pads or tampons down the toilet
  • Use designated disposal bins when available
  • Wash hands after disposing of products
  • Keep disposal bags for when bins aren't available

Clothing & Comfort

  • Wear breathable cotton underwear
  • Change underwear daily during your period
  • Avoid tight-fitting clothes during heavy flow days
  • Keep extra underwear and products with you
  • Wash period underwear in cold water

What to Avoid

  • Don't use scented products on your vulva
  • Avoid douching - it disrupts natural balance
  • Don't leave tampons in for more than 8 hours
  • Avoid using super absorbent products unnecessarily
  • Don't ignore unusual odors or symptoms

Why Menstrual Hygiene Matters

Understanding the importance of cleanliness during your menstrual cycle

Health Benefits

  • •Prevents bacterial and fungal infections
  • •Reduces risk of urinary tract infections (UTIs)
  • •Prevents toxic shock syndrome (TSS)
  • •Maintains healthy pH balance

Comfort & Confidence

  • •Eliminates unpleasant odors
  • •Prevents skin irritation and rashes
  • •Allows you to stay active and social
  • •Boosts overall well-being

Connection to Overall Health

Your menstrual health is closely linked to your overall well-being. Good hygiene habits during your period help:

  • • Maintain your body's natural defense systems
  • • Support your immune system
  • • Promote better sleep and mood
  • • Build healthy lifelong habits

Warning Signs to Watch For:

See a healthcare provider if you experience: severe pain, very heavy bleeding, strong or fishy odors, fever, or unusual discharge. These could be signs of infection or other conditions.

Understanding Your Reproductive System

Learn about the anatomy and science behind menstruation

Female Reproductive System

Organ Functions

Uterus

A pear-shaped organ where a baby can grow. The inner lining (endometrium) thickens each month and is shed during menstruation if pregnancy doesn't occur.

Ovaries

Two small organs that store eggs and produce hormones like estrogen and progesterone. They release one egg each month during ovulation.

Fallopian Tubes

Tubes that connect the ovaries to the uterus. The egg travels through these tubes after being released from the ovary.

Cervix

The narrow lower part of the uterus that connects to the vagina. It produces mucus that changes throughout your cycle.

Vagina

A muscular tube that connects the cervix to the outside of the body. This is where menstrual blood flows out.

The Menstrual Cycle

The menstrual cycle is a natural process that prepares your body for the possibility of pregnancy each month. It typically lasts 28 days but can range from 21-35 days.

Days 1-5: Menstruation

The lining of the uterus (endometrium) is shed through the vagina. This is your period.

Days 1-13: Follicular Phase

Your body prepares to release an egg. Hormones signal the ovaries to prepare an egg for release.

Day 14: Ovulation

A mature egg is released from the ovary and travels down the fallopian tube.

Days 15-28: Luteal Phase

The uterine lining thickens in preparation for a possible pregnancy. If pregnancy doesn't occur, the cycle begins again.

Key Reproductive Organs

Ovaries

Two small organs that store and release eggs, and produce hormones like estrogen and progesterone.

Fallopian Tubes

Two tubes that connect the ovaries to the uterus. This is where fertilization typically occurs.

Uterus

A pear-shaped organ where a fertilized egg can develop. Its lining sheds during menstruation.

Cervix

The lower part of the uterus that opens into the vagina. It produces mucus that changes throughout your cycle.

Vagina

A muscular canal that connects the cervix to the outside of the body. Menstrual fluid exits through here.

Important Hormones

  • Estrogen: Helps build up the uterine lining and regulates the menstrual cycle
  • Progesterone: Maintains the uterine lining and prepares it for pregnancy
  • FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone): Stimulates egg development in the ovaries
  • LH (Luteinizing Hormone): Triggers ovulation

Ask a Doctor - Q&A with Dr. Vani Venkatachalam

Common questions answered by a pediatrician specializing in adolescent health

Q: At what age do most girls get their first period?

Dr. Venkatachalam: Most girls get their first period between ages 10-15, with the average being around 12-13 years old. It's completely normal for it to happen earlier or later than this range.

Q: How much bleeding is normal during a period?

Dr. Venkatachalam: Normal period flow varies greatly between individuals. Generally, losing 30-40ml of fluid over 3-7 days is typical. If you're changing your pad or tampon every hour for several hours, consult a healthcare provider.

Q: Is it normal to have irregular periods when you first start?

Dr. Venkatachalam: Yes, it's very normal! It can take 1-2 years for your menstrual cycle to become regular after your first period. Your body is still learning and adjusting to hormonal changes.

Q: What can I do about menstrual cramps?

Dr. Venkatachalam: Mild to moderate cramps are normal. Try heat therapy, gentle exercise, over-the-counter pain relievers (with parent permission), and relaxation techniques. Severe pain that interferes with daily activities should be evaluated by a doctor.

Q: Can I swim or exercise during my period?

Dr. Venkatachalam: Absolutely! Physical activity can actually help reduce cramps and improve mood. For swimming, tampons or menstrual cups work best. Listen to your body and rest if needed.

Q: When should I see a doctor about my period?

Dr. Venkatachalam: Consult a healthcare provider if you experience: periods lasting longer than 7 days, extremely heavy bleeding, severe pain, periods more frequent than every 21 days or less frequent than every 35 days after the first year, or any concerning symptoms.

Q: Is it normal to feel emotional before or during my period?

Dr. Venkatachalam: Yes, hormonal changes can affect mood. Many people experience mood swings, irritability, or feeling more emotional. However, if these feelings significantly impact your daily life, it's worth discussing with a healthcare provider.

Q: What foods should I eat during my period?

Dr. Venkatachalam: Focus on iron-rich foods (leafy greens, lean meats), calcium sources (dairy, fortified foods), and plenty of water. Limit excessive caffeine and sugar, which can worsen mood swings and cramps.

Q: How do I know if I have a menstrual disorder?

Dr. Venkatachalam: Warning signs include: no period by age 16, periods stopping for 3+ months without pregnancy, bleeding between periods, periods lasting more than 7 days, or pain so severe it interferes with daily activities.

Q: Can stress affect my menstrual cycle?

Dr. Venkatachalam: Yes, stress can significantly impact your cycle, causing periods to be late, early, heavier, lighter, or even skipped. Managing stress through healthy coping strategies can help regulate your cycle.

A Note from Dr. Venkatachalam

"Remember, every person's menstrual experience is unique. Don't hesitate to ask questions and seek help when needed. Your menstrual health is an important part of your overall well-being, and there's no question too small or embarrassing to ask a healthcare provider."
