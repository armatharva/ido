Menstrual Products Comparison

Learn about different menstrual products and their pros and cons.

Pads

Pros:

  • Easy to use
  • Available everywhere
  • No insertion required
  • Good for beginners

Cons:

  • Can feel bulky
  • May shift during activity
  • Need frequent changes
  • Not good for swimming

Tampons

Pros:

  • Comfortable when inserted correctly
  • Great for swimming and sports
  • Discreet
  • No feeling of wetness

Cons:

  • Learning curve for insertion
  • TSS risk if left too long
  • Can be expensive
  • May cause dryness

Menstrual Cups

Pros:

  • Eco-friendly and reusable
  • Cost-effective long term
  • Can wear for up to 12 hours
  • No odor

Cons:

  • Steep learning curve
  • Requires proper sterilization
  • Initial cost
  • Can be messy to empty

Menstrual Hygiene Advice

Essential hygiene practices to keep you healthy and comfortable during your period.

Change Regularly

Change your pad every 4-6 hours, or tampons every 4-8 hours to prevent infection and odor.

Wash Your Hands

Always wash your hands before and after changing menstrual products.

Clean Gently

Wash your genital area with warm water. Avoid harsh soaps or douching.

Proper Disposal

Wrap used products in toilet paper or their wrapper and dispose in trash, never flush.

Emergency Kit Essentials

  • Extra pads or tampons
  • Spare underwear
  • Wet wipes
  • Small plastic bags for disposal
  • Pain relief medication
  • Heating pad or hot water bottle

Why Menstrual Hygiene Matters

Understanding the importance of cleanliness during your menstrual cycle.

Health Benefits

  • Prevents bacterial and fungal infections
  • Reduces risk of urinary tract infections (UTIs)
  • Prevents toxic shock syndrome (TSS)
  • Maintains healthy pH balance

Comfort & Confidence

  • Eliminates unpleasant odors
  • Prevents skin irritation and rashes
  • Allows you to stay active and social
  • Boosts overall well-being

Warning Signs to Watch For:

See a healthcare provider if you experience: severe pain, very heavy bleeding, strong or fishy odors, fever, or unusual discharge. These could be signs of infection or other conditions.

Understanding Your Reproductive System

Learn about your body and how menstruation works.

Female Reproductive System Diagram

Source: Planned Parenthood - Reproductive System

Organ Functions

Uterus

A pear-shaped organ where a baby can grow. The inner lining (endometrium) thickens each month and is shed during menstruation if pregnancy doesn't occur.

Ovaries

Two small organs that store eggs and produce hormones like estrogen and progesterone. They release one egg each month during ovulation.

Fallopian Tubes

Tubes that connect the ovaries to the uterus. The egg travels through these tubes after being released from the ovary.

Cervix

The narrow lower part of the uterus that connects to the vagina. It produces mucus that changes throughout your cycle.

Vagina

A muscular tube that connects the cervix to the outside of the body. This is where menstrual blood flows out.

Ask a Doctor

Common questions about menstruation answered by pediatrician Dr. Vani Venkatachalam of Care & Cure Pediatrics.

⚠️ Emergency Needs immediate medical attention
⏰ Concerning Should be monitored and discussed with healthcare provider
✅ Normal Common questions about normal menstrual experiences
Normal 1. Why are my periods so different from my friends? +

Every girl's body is different. Some have shorter periods than others; some bleed less and some bleed more. Go to the doctor if your period is excessively painful, if it lasts longer than seven days or makes you skip school. These are signs of a hormonal imbalance or excessive menstrual bleeding.

Concerning 2. Is it bad if I skip a month sometimes? +

Period skipping is normal, especially for the first few years. Your body is still adjusting to it all. Still, if you miss for several months or experience accompanying symptoms like a weight change or hirsutism, it wouldn't hurt to see a physician.

Concerning 3. Why does my mood get really bad before my period? +

This is probably premenstrual syndrome or PMS. Right before your period, your hormones drop and this may cause sadness, anger, anxiety. You can try journaling, talking to someone, and light exercise such as walking or stretching. If you're concerned, mention it to a physician; it could be PMDD which is a more extreme situation, but something that can be treated.

Emergency 4. I feel super tired during my period. Is that normal? +

Yes, that is normal. You're losing blood and your body's working overtime. Make sure you eat iron-rich foods (beans, leafy greens, fortified cereals) and hydrate. However, if you feel faint or lightheaded frequently, a physician might check your blood iron status.

Emergency 5. Sometimes I get clumps or clots in my blood—is that bad? +

Small clots can be normal (dime-sized or smaller). Blood gets old and you're shedding pieces of tissue. If you constantly pass large clots, however, or find yourself soaking pads in minutes, see a doctor as this can mean excessive menstrual bleeding.

Normal 6. Am I able to swim or participate in sports on my period? +

Yes. You shouldn't allow your period to hold you back from anything. Swimming is made easier with tampons or menstrual cups, and it's no issue to use a pad for other things. Even cramping can be relieved by exercise.

Concerning 7. Why am I sometimes left with an odour when I am on my period? +

Some smell is okay, it's just blood and normal bacteria. But if they smell really strong, it could be a sign of an illness or if your pad has not been changed often enough. Consult a doctor if the smell is pungent or fishy, and attempt to switch the pads on a regular basis.

Normal 8. Is it okay to be embarrassed to have my period? +

Totally normal, but you don't have to be. Every girl gets their period, and it's a normal and healthy part of life. Talking about it makes the stigma smaller.

Concerning 9. What do I do if I don't have any pads or tampons—what can I use? +

They can be used with soft, clean towels folded thick enough to soak up flow, like cotton cloth or worn-out T-shirts. Keep them clean in a clean bag after washing them with soap and water and, if possible at all, drying out in the sun. This is referred to as the use of reused cloth pads, and done the right way, it's safe.

Normal 10. How do I stay clean if I cannot shower every day? +

Gently wash your private area from the front to the back with a clean, wet cloth saturated with water once or twice a day. Wash only the outside. Wearing pantyhose or underwear made of a breathable material is also useful.

Concerning 11. How do I keep the same cloth or pad on all day? +

Change it every 6 to 8 hours if at all possible. To minimize irritation, fold over a fresh layer of cloth over the soiled one if you can't. Wash or rinse as soon as you can. Having one pad on for extended periods can lead to infection or rash.

Normal 12. What do I do if I lack products or am too shy to ask? +

Free period products are available at some schools, community centers, shelters, and even food pantries; these are usually low-key and not judged. You may be able to receive assistance from a teacher, school nurse, or other adult if you are comfortable with them. Many others want to help.

Emergency 13. Why do my cramps worsen some months? +

Cramps happen when your uterus muscles contract to shed its lining. Other times your stress or hormonal level may make them worse. You can relieve them with exercise, a heat pack, and hydration. If cramps force you to vomit or miss school regularly, that's not normal—see a doctor.

Normal 14. Why do I get bloated or puffy before my period? +

The hormonal changes cause water retention in your body. It is temporary. Drinking water and reducing super salty foods can help.

Concerning 15. What do I do if my period bleeds through my clothes at school? +

It happens to us all. If you can, wrap a sweatshirt around your waist and go pee to change your pad or insert additional toilet paper temporarily. If you can, bring an additional set of underwear with you.

Normal 16. Is it wrong that I use toilet paper instead of a pad for a while? +

It's okay as an extremely short-term solution, but it will not absorb well and will deteriorate. Change it often and switch to a pad or clean cloth as quickly as possible.

Normal 17. What if I don't have a location where I can change my pad or cloth in private? +

Try to make arrangements for times when you can have some privacy, even if just for a few minutes. Public restrooms, school nurse's offices, or even standing in an alleyway with a friend standing guard can help.

Concerning 18. How do I avoid rashes when changing isn't an option? +

If you can't change your pad often, putting a thin layer of petroleum jelly (if you have it) onto the skin will reduce friction. Wearing loose clothes also facilitates air circulation.

Concerning 19. What if I can't wash my hands before or after changing? +

If you can't access soap and water, at least apply hand sanitizer before and after. If you can't have those, try to touch only the clean side of pads or cloths and wash as soon as possible after.

Normal 20. How do I keep my reusable cloths clean if I don't have the ability to dry them in the sun? +

Sunlight is most effective in killing bacteria, but if that is not available, dry them in the cleanest, driest spot you can think of—such as hanging them in a well-ventilated area. Dry them thoroughly before reusing to prevent infections.
